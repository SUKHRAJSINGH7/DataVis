Exploring the Impact of Cost on Higher Education

By Sukhraj Singh

This project explores how the rising cost of higher education in the United States is affecting students and society. Using interactive data visualizations, we examine tuition trends, enrollment shifts, post-graduation debt, and the economic return on different degrees.

Tuition Growth Over Time

The cost of attending college in the United States has exploded over the last 60 years. In the early 1960s, average annual tuition at all institutions was just over $1,200. Today, that number has soared past $27,000. This chart shows just how steep and unrelenting that climb has been. While inflation plays a role, tuition has increased at a rate far beyond general cost-of-living adjustments, outpacing wage growth, housing, and even healthcare. For many families, affording college without borrowing is no longer possible. What was once a manageable expense has now become one of the largest financial commitments a person can take on before age 20.

Student Debt Over Time (Undergraduate vs Graduate)

As tuition climbed, so did borrowing. This chart breaks down how student debt has grown over time — not just in total, but split between undergraduate and graduate borrowers. Undergraduate debt has steadily risen, but graduate debt is increasing at an even faster pace, especially as students pursue high-cost degrees like law, business, or medicine. These are not just bigger numbers — they represent people entering adulthood tens or even hundreds of thousands of dollars in the red. For many, debt now stretches well into their 30s and beyond, shaping decisions about careers, housing, and family planning.

Breakdown of Average American Debt

Student loans are no longer just a temporary hurdle — they’ve become a defining part of the American debt profile. This chart breaks down the average American’s total debt by category, from mortgages to credit cards. Student loans now sit alongside the biggest liabilities most people carry in their lives. What makes student debt different is timing. Most borrowers take it on before their first full-time job, long before buying a house or a car. And unlike other forms of debt, student loans often don’t come with assets — just a degree, which may or may not pay off. For a growing number of people, this is the first major financial commitment they make — and it lingers for decades.

Cost of Living Index by State (2025)

Student debt doesn’t exist in a vacuum — it plays out differently depending on where you live. This map shows the cost of living index by state, highlighting how far your money actually goes after graduation. In high-cost states like California, Massachusetts, or New York, even a solid salary can feel tight once you factor in rent, groceries, and healthcare. The same student loan payment feels heavier when everything else around it costs more too. On the other hand, states with lower living costs may offer more breathing room, but often come with fewer job opportunities in high-paying sectors. The reality is that geography now plays a major role in how manageable — or unmanageable — student debt becomes..

College Enrollment Trends Over Time

For decades, college enrollment in the U.S. climbed steadily. But that growth has now stalled — and in recent years, it’s started to reverse. This chart shows the slow but clear decline in the number of students enrolling in college. The reasons are layered: rising tuition, fear of lifelong debt, and growing skepticism about whether a degree truly guarantees better opportunities. For many, the risk-reward equation of higher education has changed. More students are now questioning whether college is worth it — and increasingly, they’re deciding it’s not.

Homeownership Rate by Age Group (2025)

Student debt impacts financial independence and long-term stability. This chart breaks down homeownership rates by age group as of 2025. Younger Americans—especially those under 35—are significantly less likely to own homes. The financial strain from rising tuition costs and student loans delays key milestones like buying a house, starting a family, or saving for retirement. These delays aren't just temporary setbacks—they contribute to long-term disparities in wealth and security between generations, with younger adults falling further behind.
